Sour Punch

Ocean acidification is no lemonade.

We all know that global warming leads to temperature increase of ocean, which can be a big deal because it also results in more ice melting in the Polar Regions and deteriorate the problem even further (look up the Albedo page for more details). In addition, global warming is a big culprit of ocean acidification.

Let us take a step back, global warming is driven by greenhouse gas emissions. Among all kinds of greenhouse gases, carbon dioxide make up roughly 80 percent of the total. Therefore, global warming is greatly affected by the emission of carbon dioxide, so does the ocean, which takes up about 70% of the earth surface area.

According to UCSDâ€™s Scripps Institution of Oceanography, Ocean has absorbed 26% of the carbon dioxide produced by burning fuels. It is a major carbon sink in earth, which is a very good thing for human because it keeps plenty of CO2 out of the atmosphere. However, when CO2 reacts with seawater, it releases hydrogen and therefore increases the acidity of seawater. In fact, the acidity of ocean has increased by a third since industrial revolution. If we continue to produce carbon dioxide as what we are currently doing, the acidity of seawater will double before 2100.

The question comes, why does ocean acidification matter? Thousands of ocean creatures form protective shells to survive. They make their shells by using molecules called carbonate ions from seawater. With the increase of hydrogen ions, the carbonate ions are depleted. Related ocean creatures find it more difficult to produce shells, maintain energy to find food, reproduce and survive. To make it short, shell-forming animals dissolve when acidity gets very high. One of the specific examples is that the life forms like shrimps, lobsters and crabs in the US west coast are at risk because of the acidity increase in seawater.

At the first glance, it seems to be a problem for only the shell-forming creatures. However, it is actually a very serious issue because if the smallest things in the ocean are affected by ocean acidification, it ripples all the way up the food web, according to Dr. Palumbi from Stanford University. It means that the food web will become more vulnerable. More kinds of ocean creatures will be prone to extinction and the marine biodiversity will be harmed.

In addition, ocean acidification will negatively affect the living environment for ocean creatures. Coral reefs are one of the big victims because they are mainly structured by calcium carbonate. Ocean acidification will corrode the structure and put ocean species that live in coral reefs (which take up a quarter of the total) in jeopardy.
